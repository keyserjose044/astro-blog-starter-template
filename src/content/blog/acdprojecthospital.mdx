üß∑ Hand-Designed Corbel Supporting a Precast Beam

One of the second-floor beams is modeled as precast. At each supporting column the beam‚Äôs end reaction is carried by a reinforced concrete corbel.

The analysis model gives:

    Factored vertical reaction at the corbel:

        Vu (kips) at a distance a (inches) from the column face

    The vertical dimension h of the corbel (for example, 18 in)

    The column / corbel width bw (for example, 14 in)

We assume a small horizontal tension force at the top of the corbel, Nuc, to account for effects such as creep and shrinkage between the beam and column.

    Note: In the classroom example this horizontal force was taken as
    Nuc = 0.2 √ó (service dead reaction) √ó 1.6.
    The same approach can be used here, or as directed by the instructor.

Below is the design checklist for the corbel.
1) Geometry and Applicability

    Choose corbel overall depth h (for example, 18 in).

    Place the main tension steel approximately 1 in from the top surface ‚Üí effective depth d ‚âà h ‚àí 1 in.

    Check:

        a / d < 1.0

        Depth of concrete at the corbel face under the bearing plate ‚â• 0.5 d

If these are satisfied, the ACI 318 corbel provisions are applicable.
2) Concrete Shear Limit

The code places an upper bound on the nominal shear strength Vn. Compute these three expressions:

    V1 = 0.2 √ó f'c √ó bw √ó d

    V2 = 1600 √ó bw √ó d

    V3 = (480 + 0.08 √ó f'c) √ó bw √ó d

Use f'c in psi, bw and d in inches, giving V in pounds.
The smallest of V1, V2, V3 is Vn,max.

Check:

    phi √ó Vn,max ‚â• Vu

with phi = 0.75 for shear in this region.
3) Shear-Friction Steel Avf

To provide shear transfer across the corbel‚Äìcolumn interface, we use the shear-friction concept:

    Take coefficient of friction Œº = 1.4 for a monolithically cast interface.

    Required shear-friction steel area:

        Avf = Vu / (phi √ó Œº √ó fy)

This area is usually provided by the same bars that serve as the main tension reinforcement, as long as they cross the assumed shear-friction plane and are properly anchored.
4) Factored Moment at Column Face

The corbel behaves like a short cantilever. The design moment at the column face is:

    Mu = Vu √ó a + Nuc √ó (h ‚àí d)

The second term is often small if h ‚àí d is about 1 in, but it is included for completeness.
5) Flexural Steel for Mu (Af)

For a quick and accurate classroom design, the required steel ratio can be approximated as:

    œÅ ‚âà Mu / (phi √ó fy √ó bw √ó d¬≤)

where:

    Mu is in in-kips (convert to in-lb if needed),

    bw and d are in inches,

    fy is in psi,

    phi is the flexural strength reduction factor (often 0.9 for flexure, or 0.75 in some corbel examples).

Then:

    Af = œÅ √ó bw √ó d

Af is the steel area needed to resist the bending moment.
6) Extra Steel for Horizontal Tension (An)

To resist the axial tension Nuc at the top:

    An = Nuc / (phi √ó fy)

This area is added to the flexural requirement.
7) Total Main Steel Asc

ACI requires the total main tension steel Asc to be at least the largest of three expressions:

    Af + An

    (2 / 3) √ó Avf + An

    0.04 √ó (f'c / fy) √ó bw √ó d

Compute all three, then take:

    Asc,required = maximum of (1, 2, 3)

Choose a convenient bar arrangement (for example 2 #6 bars, or 3 #7 bars) such that:

    Asc,provided ‚â• Asc,required

These bars run near the top of the corbel, welded or otherwise anchored to the bearing plate or angle at the tip and extended into the column.
8) Closed Hoops (Ah)

The corbel also needs closed hoop reinforcement whose legs are parallel to the main tension bars.
ACI gives:

    Ah = 0.5 √ó (Asc ‚àí An)

Important detail: for each closed stirrup, both legs parallel to the main bars count.
For example, a #3 bar has area 0.11 in¬≤.
One closed #3 hoop has two such legs ‚Üí 0.22 in¬≤ contributing to Ah.

Provide enough hoops such that:

    Ah,provided ‚â• Ah,required

Place these hoops within approximately the upper two-thirds of the depth measured from the tension steel. Spacings around 3 to 4 in are typical.
Small ‚Äúframing bars‚Äù in the corner can be added to improve anchorage and detailing.
9) Development and Anchorage of Main Bars

The main bars must develop their tensile force within the column:

    Use ACI development-length rules for hooked bars in tension.

    For a given bar size and f'c = 5 ksi, a tabulated basic hooked-bar development length can be used (for example, values from CRSI or design aids).

    If the provided steel area is greater than required, ACI allows the development length to be reduced in proportion (down to a prescribed minimum factor).

In practice, this means extending the bars well into the column with a standard 90-degree hook and ensuring enough straight length plus hook is available inside the column core.
10) Bearing Under the Plate

Finally, check that the bearing plate does not overstress the concrete:

    Let A1 be the area of the bearing plate in contact with the concrete.

    Let A2 be the effective supporting area behind that bearing (often the column area under the plate).

    Nominal bearing strength:

        Pn = 0.85 √ó f'c √ó A1 √ó sqrt(A2 / A1)

    Design strength: phi √ó Pn, with phi for bearing (commonly 0.65 or as specified).

Check:

    phi √ó Pn ‚â• Vu

If needed, adjust the plate size or provide local confinement.

Plate thickness and welds/anchors are then checked using simple steel design principles.
